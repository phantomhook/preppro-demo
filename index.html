Informed Consent

In California, patients have the right to make informed decisions about their own healthcare. Before any medical procedure or treatment, healthcare workers must ensure the patient has given informed consent — a process that goes beyond simply signing a form.

💡 California law (H&S Code §72527) requires healthcare facilities to provide patients with a written statement of their rights, including the right to informed consent, before receiving care.

What Makes Consent "Informed"?

For consent to be legally valid in California, the patient must be given four key elements of information:

DiagnosisWhat condition is being treated or evaluated
Proposed TreatmentWhat procedure or intervention is recommended
Risks & BenefitsPotential outcomes — positive and negative
AlternativesOther treatment options, including doing nothing

Who Can Give Consent?

An adult (18+) with decision-making capacity can give consent for themselves. If a patient lacks capacity — due to unconsciousness, cognitive impairment, or age — consent must be obtained from an authorized representative: a legal guardian, healthcare proxy, or in emergencies, consent may be implied.

⚠️ A patient can withdraw consent at any time, even after a procedure has begun. Always document consent and any withdrawal clearly in the patient record.

Question 1 of 3
Which of the following is NOT a required element of informed consent under California law?
AThe patient's insurance coverage information
BThe risks and benefits of the proposed treatment
CAvailable alternative treatments
DThe diagnosis or condition being treated
✅ Correct! Insurance information is not part of the four required elements of informed consent.
❌ Not quite. Insurance coverage is NOT a required element — the four required elements are: diagnosis, proposed treatment, risks/benefits, and alternatives.
Question 2 of 3
A patient signs a consent form but later changes their mind during the procedure. What should the healthcare worker do?
AStop the procedure and respect the patient's right to withdraw consent
BContinue since the form was already signed
CAsk the patient's family for permission to continue
DFinish quickly to minimize disruption to the patient
✅ Correct! Patients may withdraw consent at any time. Always stop and document the withdrawal.
❌ Incorrect. A signed form does not remove the patient's right to withdraw. Stop the procedure immediately and document everything.
Question 3 of 3
Who may give consent for an unconscious adult patient brought to the ER with no family present?
AThe attending nurse on duty
BNo one — treatment must wait until the patient regains consciousness
CImplied consent applies — emergency treatment may proceed
DThe hospital administrator
✅ Correct! In emergencies where the patient cannot consent and no representative is available, implied consent allows treatment to proceed to preserve life.
❌ Not quite. In a life-threatening emergency, implied consent allows healthcare workers to treat the patient without waiting for explicit consent.
